A man is deported to a country where a federal judge has explicitly ordered he cannot be sent. A defense secretary shares active military strike coordinates with his wife and his lawyer on a consumer messaging app. A cost-cutting initiative intended to shrink government instead crashes the systems that 70 million Americans depend on for their Social Security checks. A tariff regime announced as protecting American workers produces the lowest job growth since the Great Recession. A single air traffic controller, left to juggle both ground and air traffic alone, clears a fire truck onto an active runway and then screams for it to stop as a jet barrels toward it at 104 miles per hour. And a military launches strikes against a nation whose most predictable retaliatory move is mining the world's most critical oil chokepoint — after retiring the only ships capable of clearing those mines from the theater five months earlier.

These are not isolated failures. They are symptoms of something far more dangerous: an administration that has made a governing philosophy out of not thinking ahead. And unlike the rhetoric, unlike the branding, unlike the rallies..the consequences of that philosophy are not theoretical. They are happening now, to real people, with real costs that we will be paying for decades.

## The Deportation Machine Without a Manual

On March 15, 2025, the Trump administration loaded Kilmar Abrego Garcia onto a plane and flew him to El Salvador's CECOT mega-prison. Abrego Garcia had a standing federal order — a withholding of removal — that specifically prohibited his deportation to El Salvador because of the mortal danger he would face there. He had a wife and three children who are U.S. citizens. The administration sent him anyway.

The Supreme Court ruled unanimously that the government must facilitate his return. Chief Justice Roberts wrote: "The United States acknowledges that Abrego Garcia was subject to a withholding order forbidding his removal to El Salvador, and that the removal to El Salvador was therefore illegal." U.S. District Judge Paula Xinis accused the administration of "bad faith" and weighed contempt charges for non-compliance. No basis in law. Not a contested legal theory. Not a gray area. No basis at all.

And Abrego Garcia was not an anomaly. NPR reported in February 2026 that "the problem is getting worse." Hondurans and Salvadorans have reported learning they were being deported to Guatemala only after they were already in the air. A Guatemalan man was deported to Mexico two days after telling a court he had been abducted and raped there. The administration has sent non-Cameroonians to Cameroon, non-Ecuadorans to Ecuador, and non-Hondurans to Honduras. The advocacy coalition Home is Here has tracked at least 22 instances of people with valid DACA status being swept up in arrests, placed in detention, or deported.

Then there is the Alien Enemies Act — an 1798 wartime statute that has not been invoked since the internment of Japanese, German, and Italian nationals during World War II. The administration dusted it off to target members of Tren de Aragua, a Venezuelan prison gang, authorizing expedited removal of all Venezuelan citizens 14 and older deemed to be members. ProPublica investigated and found that the vast majority of men sent to CECOT had no criminal records, and none appeared on Interpol's or Venezuelan law enforcement's lists of known gang members. Among them was Jerce Reyes Barrios, a professional soccer player and coach seeking asylum after being tortured in Venezuela. DHS officers designated him a gang member because they misidentified his Real Madrid soccer team tattoo and an "I love you" sign language gesture in a social media photo as gang signs. A Real Madrid tattoo. That was the evidence.

District judges in Texas, Colorado, and New York struck the Act down. The Fifth Circuit blocked it, ruling that the administration's claim of a "warlike invasion" was "not true." The Supreme Court intervened twice — not to uphold the policy, but to ensure that people being deported under it had more than a few hours to argue against their own removal. In December 2025, a federal judge ruled the deportations violated due process.

But the deportation machine was only half of it. The enforcement machine came home — and it came for American citizens.

On his first day in office, Trump rescinded the decade-old bipartisan policy that had protected schools, churches, hospitals, and other sensitive locations from immigration raids. For more than ten years, under administrations of both parties, ICE had voluntarily refrained from enforcement at places where fear of arrest would deter people from seeking education, medical care, or worship. That restraint was eliminated with a signature. ICE now operates in schools, places of worship, hospitals, daycares, and funeral homes.

What followed was predictable to anyone who has ever studied the history of unchecked enforcement power turned loose without guardrails. In Chicago, Operation Midway Blitz launched in September 2025, deploying hundreds of federal agents across Illinois. DHS arrested more than 4,300 people over two months. Federal data later revealed that two-thirds of those detained in the first half of the operation had no criminal convictions or pending charges. A 2,450 percent increase in the number of people with no criminal record held in ICE detention — that is not a typo. Advocates documented Latino U.S. citizens racially profiled by federal agents. At least 10 American citizens were falsely charged with federal crimes during the operation; the charges were later dropped or dismissed. A U.S. citizen named Angel was grabbed off the street in the South Chicago neighborhood by Border Patrol agents and pulled into an unmarked vehicle — the incident captured on his cousin's Ring doorbell camera. After interrogating him, agents dropped him off a half mile away. No explanation. No apology. A federal judge had to issue a temporary restraining order over detention conditions at the Broadview facility, citing a lack of bedding, toiletries, medication, and regular meals.

In Newark, Mayor Ras Baraka was arrested on May 9, 2025 — handcuffed by ICE agents wearing face coverings while protesters cried "Shame" — for allegedly trespassing at an ICE detention center. He had been attempting to enter the facility alongside three members of New Jersey's congressional delegation to conduct oversight. The charges were later dismissed. The federal magistrate who heard the case called Baraka's arrest "hasty" and "a worrying misstep."

Then came Minneapolis. On December 1, 2025, DHS began deploying large numbers of ICE and CBP personnel to the Twin Cities. On January 5, 2026, 2,000 additional federal agents arrived — and DHS announced Operation Metro Surge as the largest immigration enforcement operation ever carried out. Over the course of two months, approximately 4,000 people were arrested. ICE's own data showed that only 5 percent had records of violent crimes.

On January 7, two days into the expanded deployment, Renée Nicole Macklin Good was shot and killed by ICE agent Jonathan Ross. Good was 37. A mother of three. A U.S. citizen. She had just dropped her six-year-old off at school and was observing ICE activity with her wife. Video analysis from three angles showed her vehicle turning away from the officer when he opened fire. Her last words to Ross, captured on recording: "That's fine, dude; I'm not mad at you." Three shots. She was dead.

The federal response: Vice President Vance called her death "a tragedy of her own making." DHS Secretary Kristi Noem labeled this mother of three — who had just come from her daughter's school — a "domestic terrorist."

On January 14, a Venezuelan man named Julio Sosa-Celis was shot in the leg by an ICE agent. On January 24, it happened again. Alex Jeffrey Pretti, a 37-year-old ICU nurse at a Veterans Affairs hospital and a U.S. citizen, was at a protest against the operation when he saw a federal agent push a woman to the ground. He stepped between them, put his arm around her. He was holding his cell phone in one hand. His other hand was empty. Agents pepper sprayed him, then six of them wrestled him to the ground. Two CBP officers fired approximately ten shots. Pretti had a legal permit to carry. His gun was in his waistband. He never drew it. It was removed from him while he was on the ground. The administration claimed he was "brandishing" a weapon and attempting to "massacre law enforcement." The video shows no such thing. His father said his son was "a kind-hearted soul" whose "last thought and act was to protect a woman."

One person detained during the operation died in ICE custody. Another — Victor Manuel Diaz, an immigrant from Nicaragua swept up in Metro Surge — was found dead at a Texas detention facility. ICE said he appeared to have killed himself.

On January 23, an estimated 50,000 people marched through downtown Minneapolis in subzero temperatures. A coalition of Minnesota labor unions endorsed the protest, combining it with a general strike and statewide business closures by hundreds of businesses in solidarity — potentially the first general strike in the United States since the Taft-Hartley Act of 1947. Tens of thousands more protested nationally after Good's death.

Governor Tim Walz called the operation "a campaign of organized brutality" and noted the federal government had blocked state investigations. Minneapolis Mayor Jacob Frey demanded ICE withdrawal and accused agents of being poorly trained and dangerous. Chris Madel, a Republican gubernatorial candidate, withdrew from the 2026 race, stating: "I cannot support the national Republicans' stated retribution on the citizens of our state." When a Republican candidate in Minnesota drops out of a race because his own party's federal agents are killing his constituents, you have lost the plot entirely.

Trump's response to the protests was to threaten to invoke the Insurrection Act. Fifteen hundred active-duty troops were placed on standby. The governor of a state was being threatened with military occupation because his citizens objected to federal agents killing their neighbors.

Minnesota Attorney General Keith Ellison, alongside the cities of Minneapolis and Saint Paul, filed suit against DHS, alleging violations of the First, Fourth, and Tenth Amendments — unlawful seizures, excessive force, and retaliation against constitutionally protected speech. The ACLU filed a class-action lawsuit documenting retaliatory arrests of observers, traffic stops without reasonable suspicion, and the use of flashbangs, chemical irritants, LRADs, and beatings against peaceful demonstrators. U.S. District Judge Katherine Menendez described the consequences as "profound and even heartbreaking" — but declined to halt the operation. The operation finally ended on February 12, 2026, after more than two months. The city of Minneapolis calculated the economic impact at $203 million.

Since January 20, 2025, immigration agents have been involved in at least 33 shootings, resulting in 9 deaths. The Wall Street Journal documented at least 13 instances of agents firing at or into civilian vehicles since July 2025. Thirty-two people died in ICE custody in 2025 — the highest number since 2004. And 2026 is on track to surpass it.

This is what enforcement without forethought looks like. Not a plan to remove dangerous criminals — a dragnet that sweeps up soccer players, nurses, DACA recipients, and U.S. citizens, kills the ones who protest, and then calls the dead mother a terrorist.

And then came the consequences nobody modeled. The administration's deportation of more than half a million people, combined with the ending of legal work programs, gutted the agricultural labor force. Approximately 750,000 immigrants left the workforce since January 2025. The farm labor force alone shrank by at least 155,000 workers. In Pennsylvania, dairy farmers sold off entire herds because they could not find workers. In California, fields went unharvested because laborers stayed home out of fear of ICE raids. Trump's own Department of Labor issued a warning in October 2025 admitting the immigration crackdown risked food shortages and that American workers would not fill the gap. The administration was then forced to expand the H-2A visa program by 119,000 temporary agricultural visas — effectively acknowledging through policy what it denied in rhetoric: we needed those workers.

But the agricultural devastation does not fall equally. It never has. And this administration made certain it never will.

In 1910, Black farmers in America owned approximately 16 million acres of farmland. By 2017, that number had collapsed to 2.9 million — a loss of more than 80 percent. A 2022 study calculated the value of Black farmland lost over the 20th century at $326 billion. This did not happen by accident. Throughout the 1900s, USDA county offices systematically denied Black farmers loan applications, delayed their processing, and refused restructuring assistance that white farmers received as a matter of course. As recently as the 1990s, when Black farmers did receive loans, the average processing time was 220 days. For white farmers, it was 60 days. The result was generational destruction: families who had worked land since Reconstruction were starved of the credit and crop insurance they needed to survive, while white farmers in the same counties received both without friction.

In 2022, Congress allocated $2.2 billion through the Discrimination Financial Assistance Program to provide direct payments to farmers who had experienced documented USDA discrimination. An estimated 43,000 farmers received awards averaging $82,000 — not reparations, but a partial and overdue acknowledgment that the federal government had systematically destroyed Black agricultural wealth for a century.

The Trump administration killed it. In July 2025, the USDA filed a rule eliminating the "socially disadvantaged" farmer designation — a category established by the 1990 Farm Bill to provide outreach and technical assistance to farmers of color. The agency declared, in writing, that "past discrimination has been sufficiently addressed and that further race- and sex-based remedies are no longer necessary or legally justified under current circumstances." Three hundred and twenty-six billion dollars in stolen land. Decades of documented federal discrimination. And the official position of the United States government is that it has been "sufficiently addressed."

The National Black Farmers Association refused a subsequent $12 billion aid package, calling it "racist" for stripping the equity provisions that had been the entire point. Their president, John Boyd Jr., who has advocated for Black farmers for over 30 years, said the administration was "turning its back" on the very communities the USDA had spent a century destroying.

This is not a policy disagreement about farm subsidies. This is the deliberate dismantling of the only programs ever created to address one of the longest-running acts of institutional theft in American history. And it is part of a pattern so consistent that calling it incidental requires ignoring everything this administration has done.

Every one of these failures was predictable. Not in hindsight. In foresight. The legal vulnerabilities were obvious. The logistics were unvetted. The economic dependencies were well-documented. The human cost was foreseeable. Nobody planned. They just acted. And they acted hardest against the people who had the least.

## The Racism Is the Policy

There is a version of this essay that treats racism as a side effect — an unfortunate byproduct of an administration that governs by impulse and occasionally says ugly things. That version would be a lie.

The racism is not incidental to the governing philosophy. It is foundational. It is the organizing principle around which the impulses coalesce, the thread that connects the deportation machine to the agricultural collapse to the military purges to the dismantling of civil rights enforcement. Strip away the chaos, and what remains is a coherent project: the systematic removal of protections, recognition, and power from Black and brown Americans — carried out not in spite of the administration's agenda, but as its most consistent expression.

Start with the words, because the words always come first.

In December 2023, Donald Trump told a rally in New Hampshire that immigrants were "poisoning the blood of our country." This is not a metaphor with ambiguous origins. Adolf Hitler used the phrase "poisoning the blood" in *Mein Kampf* to describe what he claimed Jewish people were doing to the Aryan race. Trump was asked about the parallel. He did not disavow it. He repeated it. Doubling down on the overt nature of it.

In October 2024, in a radio interview with Hugh Hewitt, Trump said of immigrants accused of crimes: "It's in their genes. And we got a lot of bad genes in our country right now." This is eugenics. Not dog-whistled. Not coded. Stated plainly, on the record, by a man running for president. In 2020, at a rally in Minnesota — a state with one of the largest Somali populations in the country — he praised the nearly all-white crowd for having "good genes." His father introduced him to what he calls "racehorse theory" as a child — the belief that superior people are bred, not made. He has referenced it publicly, multiple times, without irony.

Then came Springfield.

In September 2024, Trump stood on a presidential debate stage and told 67 million viewers that Haitian immigrants in Springfield, Ohio were eating residents' pets. It was a lie. It had been manufactured and amplified for weeks by neo-Nazi groups — specifically the Blood Tribe, a white supremacist organization led by two former Marines, who had been distributing fliers and staging marches in Springfield since August. The Proud Boys descended on the city. KKK leaflets appeared on doorsteps. The Aryan Freedom Network posted recruitment fliers on utility poles. Residents found pamphlets calling Haitian immigrants "disease-ridden and filthy."

After Trump repeated the lie on national television, Springfield received over 30 bomb threats. Schools closed. Public buildings evacuated. The city's 15,000 Haitian residents — most of whom had arrived legally under Temporary Protected Status — lived in terror. The Republican governor, Mike DeWine, publicly stated that the claims were false and called for calm. It did not matter. Trump had given the neo-Nazis what they wanted: the biggest megaphone in the world repeating their talking point as fact.

And then the policy followed the rhetoric. In February 2025, DHS Secretary Kristi Noem revoked Temporary Protected Status for Haitian nationals — the second such revocation under this administration. More than 500,000 Haitian refugees were told they would lose legal status by August 2025. The same community that Trump had targeted with a manufactured lie, that had endured bomb threats and white supremacist marches as a direct result of his words, was now being stripped of the legal protection that allowed them to live and work in this country. The cruelty was not a byproduct. It was the point.

In December 2025, Trump escalated further. In a Cabinet meeting — not a rally, not an off-the-cuff remark, but a formal meeting of the President's Cabinet — he called Somali immigrants "garbage." He said of Somalia: "Their country stinks, and we don't want them in our country." He told Representative Ilhan Omar, a U.S. citizen who fled the Somali civil war as a child and lived in a Kenyan refugee camp before arriving in America at age 12, to "go back to your own country and figure out your own constitution." The next day, he continued: Somalis had "destroyed Minnesota" and "destroyed our country." Within days, Operation Metro Surge — the same operation that killed Renée Good and Alex Pretti — was deployed to the Twin Cities. It targeted the Somali community specifically. The president's rhetoric and the enforcement machine were not operating in parallel. They were operating in sequence.

But the words are only the surface. Beneath them is the institutional machinery — and this administration has been methodical in dismantling every federal mechanism that protects Black and brown Americans from discrimination.

On his first full day in office, Trump signed Executive Order 14151, "Ending Radical and Wasteful Government DEI Programs and Preferencing." Federal DEI offices were dissolved. Employees who worked in diversity and inclusion were placed on administrative leave and then terminated. At the Department of Education, at least 100 employees were purged in a single day — only two of whom had actually worked in DEI. The administration revoked nearly $900 million in education research grants, eliminating 104 of 109 grants because they allegedly "fund discriminatory practices — including in the form of DEI." The Supreme Court, by a 5-4 vote, allowed the administration to freeze an additional $65 million for teacher training.

At the Pentagon, the DEI purge took a form so grotesque it reads like satire. The Department of Defense deleted the online profile of Sergeant William Carney — the first Black American to receive the Medal of Honor, a man who carried the American flag through a hail of Confederate gunfire at Fort Wagner in 1863 — because an AI tool flagged his page as "DEI content." Fourteen of eighteen articles about Jackie Robinson's military service were removed from government websites. Stories about the Tuskegee Airmen — the Black pilots who flew 15,000 combat sorties in World War II and never lost a single bomber they escorted — were pulled offline. The Navajo code talkers, whose unbreakable language helped win the war in the Pacific, were scrubbed. Pentagon spokesperson Sean Parnell blamed the AI tool: "because of the realities of AI tools and software, some important content was incorrectly pulled offline to be reviewed." After a national outcry, some pages were restored. But the fact that the United States military pointed an AI at its own history and told it to delete anything related to diversity — and that the AI identified Black Medal of Honor recipients as the content to remove — tells you everything about what "DEI" means in this administration's vocabulary. It means Black. It means gone.

The Department of Justice completed the picture. In April 2025, the DOJ terminated an environmental justice settlement with Lowndes County, Alabama — a majority-Black county where residents have endured generations of raw sewage flowing through their yards because the local government refused to build adequate wastewater infrastructure. Children play in yards where untreated sewage pools. The DOJ's own investigation in 2023 had found a "pattern of inaction and neglect." A settlement was reached. The Trump administration killed it, citing Executive Order 14151 — the anti-DEI order. Representative Terri Sewell stated the obvious: the agreement "had nothing to do with DEI." It was about raw sewage. In Black yards. In Alabama. And the United States government walked away from it because the word "justice" in "environmental justice" had been reclassified as a prohibited concept.

The Civil Rights Division itself — the division of the Department of Justice created to enforce the Civil Rights Act, the Voting Rights Act, and the Fair Housing Act — has been "dramatically realigned," in the words of legal scholars tracking the changes. Its historic mission of enforcing anti-discrimination law has been repurposed to pursue the administration's executive orders. The division that once sued states for suppressing Black votes now investigates universities for having diversity programs.

Let's be clear about what this adds up to.

A president who quotes Hitler's language about blood purity. Who promotes eugenics on national radio. Who calls Black and brown immigrants "garbage" in Cabinet meetings and tells a Black congresswoman to go back to Africa. Whose debate performance amplified a neo-Nazi lie that triggered bomb threats against Haitian schoolchildren. Whose administration revoked legal status for half a million Haitian refugees, dismantled the only programs addressing a century of documented theft from Black farmers, deleted Black Medal of Honor recipients from military websites, terminated a settlement protecting Black children from raw sewage, gutted the Civil Rights Division, purged federal employees for working in diversity, and deployed an enforcement operation that killed American citizens — in the specific community the president had publicly called "garbage" days earlier.

This is not a list of disconnected incidents. It is a governing program. And it has a name. Every historian who studies the intersection of authoritarian nationalism, racial hierarchy, and the dismantling of institutional checks recognizes the pattern. The consolidation of executive power. The scapegoating of racial and ethnic minorities. The purging of dissent from military and civil service. The weaponization of law enforcement against domestic political opponents. The rewriting of history to erase the contributions of marginalized groups. The replacement of institutional expertise with personal loyalty. The use of mass rallies to build a cult of personality around a single leader who claims to embody the nation.

We have a word for this. Several, in fact. But the most precise one — the one that historians will use when they write about this period, whether we are comfortable with it now or not..is fascism. Not as hyperbole. Not as a rhetorical flourish. As a description of observable governance: the merger of ultranationalist ideology, racial hierarchy, and authoritarian power, executed through the machinery of a democratic state that has not yet fully realized what is being done to it.

The MAGA movement did not arrive at this destination by accident. It was built for it. From the "birther" conspiracy that launched Trump's political career — the insistence that the first Black president was secretly foreign — to the "very fine people on both sides" of Charlottesville, to the January 6th assault on the Capitol, to the current campaign of racial purges, deportation raids, and institutional demolition, the trajectory has been consistent. The base was organized around racial grievance. The movement was fueled by it. And now, with the full power of the federal government, it is being implemented as policy.

The question is no longer whether this is happening. It is whether enough of us are willing to call it what it is — and act accordingly.

## Foreign Policy as Improvisation

On February 28, 2025, Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky sat down at the White House to sign a minerals deal that both sides had spent weeks negotiating. It should have been a straightforward moment of transactional diplomacy — the kind of deal this administration claims to excel at.

It collapsed in minutes. When Zelensky stated that Putin could not be trusted and had broken previous commitments to stop fighting, Vice President Vance rebuked him: "Mr. President, with respect, I think it's disrespectful for you to come into the Oval Office to try to litigate this in front of the American media." Trump escalated: "You're gambling with the lives of millions of people. You're gambling with World War III!" Zelensky, visibly stunned, replied to Vance: "What kind of diplomacy, JD, you are speaking about? What do you mean?" The meeting ended without a signature. Trump posted on social media afterward: "He disrespected the United States of America in its cherished Oval Office." Then he suspended intelligence and military aid to Ukraine — an active warzone ally — for a week.

The deal was eventually signed two months later by subordinates — Treasury Secretary Bessent and a Ukrainian deputy prime minister — after an informal meeting at the funeral of Pope Francis. The diplomatic damage was already done.

The blowup jolted Europe into action. British Prime Minister Keir Starmer convened 18 leaders in London for crisis talks. German Chancellor Friedrich Merz announced a plan to raise hundreds of billions in defense and infrastructure spending, explicitly stating Germany must prepare for "independence" from America. French President Macron proposed extending France's nuclear deterrent to other European nations. The result was a European coalition of the willing drawing up its own peace plan — a direct response to the realization that the United States could not be relied upon to manage its own diplomacy. Our closest allies were not just disappointed. They were making contingency plans to function without us.

Meanwhile, tariffs. On April 2, 2025 — "Liberation Day" — the administration announced a 10 percent blanket tariff on all imports, 25 percent on automobiles, and reciprocal tariffs on 90 countries. China was hit with 54 percent. The EU with 20 percent. No modeling had been done. No phase-in was planned. No allies were consulted.

The market's response was immediate and catastrophic. The S&P 500 lost 4.84 percent on April 3. The next day, the Dow fell 2,231 points. In two days, over $6.6 trillion was erased — the largest two-day loss in stock market history. The administration panicked. On April 9, Trump announced a "90-day PAUSE" on most tariffs via social media post, and the S&P surged 9.5 percent in a single day. Policy by tweet, reversed by tweet.

The tariff war with China escalated through the year — from 54 percent to 104 percent to 145 percent, with China retaliating at 125 percent — before a partial reduction was negotiated in May. The average effective tariff rate reached 7.7 percent, the highest since 1947. The Tax Foundation calculated an average household burden of $1,500 annually. U.S. manufacturing contracted for 10 consecutive months. Job growth averaged 55,000 per month through November 2025, the lowest since the Great Recession outside the pandemic. Beef prices rose 16 percent. Coffee rose nearly 20 percent. Stellantis paused production and laid off 900 workers. Whirlpool cut 650 jobs at its Amana, Iowa facility. Small businesses with fewer than 10 employees lost 366,400 jobs.

American companies and consumers paid 82 percent of the tariff costs. Not China. Not Europe. Us.

Then, in February 2026, the Supreme Court ruled 6-3 in *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump* that IEEPA does not authorize the president to impose tariffs at all. Chief Justice Roberts wrote the majority opinion. The foundational legal authority for the entire tariff regime was never valid. There was no plan because there was no legal basis — and nobody checked.

And while the administration was busy alienating trade partners, it was also alienating allies on sovereignty. Trump declared of Greenland: "One way or the other, we're going to get it," and refused to rule out using the military to take it. Denmark's Prime Minister responded: "Greenland belongs to the Greenlanders." A Danish poll found 6 percent of Greenlanders support becoming part of the United States. On the Panama Canal, Trump told Congress: "My administration will be reclaiming the Panama Canal, and we've already started doing it." Panamanian President Mulino responded: "President Trump is lying. The Canal is Panamanian and will continue to be Panamanian!" These were not negotiations. They were provocations without strategy, alienating nations for headlines with no path to any outcome.

## National Security by Group Chat

On March 15, 2025 — the same day Kilmar Abrego Garcia was wrongfully deported — Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth used the Signal messaging app to share classified details of imminent airstrikes against the Houthis in Yemen, codenamed Operation Rough Rider. He shared the types of aircraft, the missiles to be used, and the launch and attack times — information taken from a USCENTCOM document classified SECRET/NOFORN. He shared them in a 19-person group chat that National Security Advisor Mike Waltz had accidentally added a journalist to — Jeffrey Goldberg, the editor-in-chief of The Atlantic. Less than 10 minutes elapsed between Hegseth receiving the information from General Kurilla and sending it to the chat.

Then it got worse. Hegseth had a second, 13-person Signal group chat where he shared the same operational details. That chat included his wife, his brother, and his personal lawyer. None of them held security clearances. None of them worked for the Defense Department.

The Pentagon inspector general concluded in December 2025 that the information Hegseth shared "matched operational information USCENTCOM sent and classified as SECRET/NOFORN," that his messages "created a risk to operational security that could have resulted in failed U.S. mission objectives and potential harm to U.S. pilots," and that he violated both protocol and federal records law by using a personal device and failing to retain the conversations. This was not a technicality. This was the Secretary of Defense sharing active strike plans on a consumer app with family members while American pilots were preparing to fly those missions. Hegseth was not fired, disciplined, or charged. The White House declared the matter "case closed."

And this was the same Pentagon that had, weeks earlier, been gutted of its senior leadership. In a single evening in February 2025, the administration fired six senior Pentagon officials — including the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, General Charles Q. Brown Jr., the first woman to lead the Navy, Admiral Lisa Franchetti, and the vice chief of the Air Force. Admiral Linda Fagan, the first woman to serve as Commandant of the Coast Guard, was also removed. No advance notice. No succession planning. By August, more than a dozen senior generals and admirals had been removed, including the directors of the NSA and the Defense Intelligence Agency. General Tim Haugh, a four-star general with a 33-year career in intelligence and cyber operations, learned of his firing with no warning. The head of the Defense Intelligence Agency, Jeffrey Kruse, was fired after a preliminary assessment of U.S. strikes on Iran's nuclear program found the damage fell short of what Trump and Netanyahu had publicly claimed.

Then Hegseth announced a plan for a minimum 20 percent reduction in four-star positions across the active-duty military. The administration's stated rationale for all of it was that the military had become too focused on diversity. The actual consequence was the hollowing out of institutional knowledge at the exact moment that the Secretary of Defense was demonstrating why institutional discipline exists.

Meanwhile, 35,000 federal troops were deployed to the southern border by September 2025 — at a cost exceeding $2 billion in the first year — while the Secretary of Defense was sharing actual combat operations on a family group chat.

## The Strait They Forgot

On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched Operation Epic Fury — surprise airstrikes across Iran that killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei and several senior Iranian officials. Within days, Iran retaliated with missile and drone strikes against Israel, U.S. bases, and allied countries across the Middle East. On March 2, the IRGC officially closed the Strait of Hormuz — the 21-mile-wide chokepoint through which one-fifth of the world's oil supply passes, roughly 13 million barrels per day. By March 10, Iran had begun laying naval mines in the shipping lanes.

None of this was surprising. Iran has maintained a mine warfare doctrine since the 1987 Tanker War. The Defense Intelligence Agency estimates Iran's mine inventory at 5,000 to 6,000 naval mines. Closing Hormuz with mines has been Iran's most obvious asymmetric retaliatory option for decades. Every war game, every strategic assessment, every congressional briefing on Iran has flagged this scenario. It is the single most predictable military response Iran could execute.

And the United States had just disarmed itself against it.

In September 2025 — five months before launching strikes on Iran — the U.S. Navy decommissioned its four Avenger-class minesweepers from Naval Support Activity Bahrain: the USS Devastator, USS Dextrous, USS Gladiator, and USS Sentry. These wooden-hulled ships had spent the entire post-Cold War era keeping the Persian Gulf's sea lanes clear. They were the only vessels in the American fleet purpose-built to find and destroy naval mines. The Navy shipped them to Philadelphia for scrap.

Their replacements — Independence-class Littoral Combat Ships carrying new Mine Countermeasures Mission packages — had only months of operational history. Pre-deployment testing revealed systemic failures: each mission required over four hours of maintenance and 1.5 hours of calibration, unmanned surface vehicles proved uncontrollable in exercises, and visual mine identification failed even in clear waters. A U.S. Navy director described the system as "troubling," admitting: "when it breaks, I'm out of a mission." Sixteen mine countermeasure vessels were deemed necessary for clearing operations. Seven were available. And two of the three LCS mine-sweeping ships — the USS Tulsa and USS Santa Barbara — were spotted not in the Persian Gulf, but thousands of miles away in Malaysia.

So where were the Arleigh Burke-class destroyers? The Navy had eight DDGs forward-deployed across the Middle East, including the USS McFaul and USS Mitscher operating near Hormuz. An Arleigh Burke carries the Aegis Combat System, 96 vertical launch cells loaded with Standard Missiles for air defense and Tomahawk cruise missiles for long-range strike. It can track and engage dozens of simultaneous threats. These are among the most capable surface combatants ever built. Why were they not positioned to interdict Iranian minelayers before mines entered the water? Why were they not providing a missile defense umbrella over the strait's approaches in the days before and after strikes that everyone knew would provoke exactly this response? Instead, the DDGs ended up spending $2 million per missile to destroy $20,000 Iranian drones — a cost asymmetry that should alarm every taxpayer — while the mines they could have helped prevent sat on the ocean floor, choking global commerce.

The consequences were immediate and catastrophic. Tanker traffic through Hormuz dropped 70 percent. Over 150 ships anchored outside the strait, refusing to transit. Oil production from Kuwait, Iraq, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE collectively dropped by more than 10 million barrels per day. Brent Crude surged past $120 per barrel. The International Energy Agency called it "the greatest global energy security challenge in history" — worse than the two 1970s oil shocks combined, worse than the disruptions from Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Gulf states, which rely on the strait for over 80 percent of their food imports, declared a grocery supply emergency. Food prices spiked 40 to 120 percent across the region. The U.S. sank 16 Iranian minelayers, including a Khadir-class submarine and a Shahid Soleimani-class corvette, but mines already in the water cannot be shot.

Trump's response to the crisis his strikes created was another impulse: a 48-hour ultimatum threatening to "obliterate" Iran's power plants if the strait was not reopened. Iran's new Supreme Leader, Mojtaba Khamenei, vowed the strait would remain closed. Trump extended the deadline by five days. Oil prices swung wildly. As of this writing, the war is in its fourth week, more than 2,000 people are dead, and the strait remains effectively closed.

You do not launch strikes against a nation with 5,000 mines and a 40-year mining doctrine without a plan to keep the world's most critical shipping lane open. You do not retire your only mine-clearing ships from the theater months before the strikes. You do not position eight destroyers in the region without tasking at least two of them to interdict minelayers in the chokepoint you know will be targeted. Unless you never thought about it. Unless the strike was the plan, and everything after it was someone else's problem.

This is the governing-by-impulse thesis made lethal on a global scale.

## DOGE: Breaking the Machine to Prove It Doesn't Work

The Department of Government Efficiency was supposed to be the crown jewel — Elon Musk's personal crusade to cut waste and shrink the federal government. He floated $2 trillion in savings during the campaign. Then it was $1 trillion. Then $150 billion. A DOGE employee, Nate Cavanaugh, stated in a January 2026 deposition that cost-cutting efforts "fell far short" of the original goal. A year in, government spending increased nearly 6 percent to $7.558 trillion. A CBS News analysis found that DOGE's cuts actually cost taxpayers an estimated $135 billion. The Cato Institute — not a liberal think tank — concluded that DOGE's failure to shrink spending despite culling more than 9 percent of the federal workforce was because most federal spending does not come from salaries.

Anyone with a spreadsheet could have told them that. Nobody checked. And the numbers they did claim were fabricated. DOGE's "Wall of Receipts" listed a single USAID contract three times at $650 million each. An SSA contract was listed as $232 million when it was actually $560,000. An ICE contract was listed as $8 billion when it was $8 million. PolitiFact, the Wall Street Journal, the New York Times, and the American Enterprise Institute all found the tallies were fiction. House DOGE caucus leader Representative Blake Moore admitted on June 5 that Republican members "always knew it was a massive exaggeration."

What DOGE did accomplish was breaking the systems that Americans depend on. The Social Security Administration, already operating at its lowest staffing levels in 50 years, saw 7,000 jobs cut from a workforce of 60,000. Its website crashed repeatedly, blocking millions from accessing their accounts. Phone wait times surged. Field offices in White Plains, Logan, Carlsbad, and elsewhere were targeted for closure. Former SSA Commissioner Martin O'Malley warned that payments to 72 million eligible Americans would be interrupted within 30 to 90 days. And then a whistleblower filed a complaint alleging that DOGE uploaded a database of Americans' sensitive Social Security information — actual Social Security numbers for over 300 million people — to an unsecured server. An unsecured server. Where have we heard that before? The same party that spent years demanding Hillary Clinton be locked up for her email server apparently sees no irony in handing 300 million Americans' Social Security numbers to Elon Musk's team on an unsecured system. At least 12 lawsuits were filed alleging Privacy Act violations involving DOGE's access to agency records. Federal judges in three districts issued injunctions blocking DOGE from accessing data at Treasury, the Education Department, and OPM.

Then there is the FAA. The agency laid off approximately 400 employees in February, targeting probationary workers in maintenance, aeronautical information, and aviation safety roles. Twelve percent of aeronautical information specialists — the people who update the charts, maps, and airspace data after disruptions, weather events, and accidents — have either left or are poised to leave. The FAA was already short approximately 3,500 air traffic controllers, with only 23 of 289 terminals meeting full staffing standards. United Airlines cancelled 35 daily round-trip flights from Newark in May 2025, citing severe FAA staffing shortages.

And then the detail that distills the entire DOGE philosophy into a single anecdote: Musk's team tried to fire air traffic controllers themselves. Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy had to personally block the terminations. In a Cabinet meeting, Duffy confronted Musk directly: "I have multiple plane crashes to deal with now, and your people want me to fire air traffic controllers?" This was weeks after an American Airlines flight collided midair with a military Black Hawk helicopter over the Potomac, killing 67 people. Musk called Duffy's account a "lie." Duffy maintained he heard it from the DOGE team directly.

And just yesterday — March 23, 2026 — the consequences arrived at LaGuardia Airport. An Air Canada Express jet carrying 76 people from Montreal collided with a Port Authority fire truck on an active runway at 104 miles per hour. Both pilots — Antoine Forest and Mackenzie Gunther — were killed. Forty-one passengers were hospitalized. Air traffic control audio captured the controller first clearing the fire truck to cross the runway, then frantically screaming "stop, stop, stop" as the jet barreled toward it. That controller was handling both ground control and air traffic control simultaneously — by himself. After the collision, he said on frequency: "I messed up." One controller. Two jobs. Two dead pilots. The NTSB investigation is ongoing, but the question writes itself: how many positions is one person supposed to cover before the system breaks?

At the NOAA and the National Weather Service, the workforce fell by more than 550 people — roughly a 10 percent cut — to below 4,000 total employees. The Houston-Galveston forecast office was left with 11 vacancies, operating at 56 percent of regular staffing heading into hurricane season. Several NWS offices stopped operating 24 hours a day. Fewer weather balloon launches meant reduced forecast accuracy. By August 2025, the NWS quietly began rehiring hundreds of positions that had been cut.

At the IRS, the Yale Budget Lab projected that if 22,000 employees left their roles, the agency would lose $8.5 billion in revenue in 2026 from reduced audit capacity. Over a decade, that loss snowballs to nearly $198 billion. IRS employees reported that mass layoffs decreased efficiency, increased call times, and slowed paperwork processing.

And then there was USAID — the agency through which the United States delivers humanitarian and development aid worldwide. Musk called it a "criminal organization" and boasted that he "spent the weekend feeding USAID into the wood chipper." The administration planned to cut over 90 percent of its contracts and reduce foreign assistance by nearly $60 billion. A study published in *The Lancet* projected these global aid cuts could lead to 9.4 million additional deaths by 2030. Over 360,000 people have already died from lack of food and medication in the wake of the cuts. HIV clinics in South Africa closed. Medical programs in Afghanistan were terminated. Seventy-five million children are projected to lose routine vaccinations.

The administration cut people. It did not cut costs. It did not modernize systems. It did not automate processes. It fired the humans who kept the machinery running and then expressed surprise when the machinery stopped. And when the Transportation Secretary tried to stop them from firing the people who keep planes from colliding, they called him a liar. Yesterday, two pilots paid the price for a system running on fumes.

Musk departed DOGE on May 30, 2025, after his 130-day special government employee term expired. His tenure was marked by legal setbacks, fabricated savings claims, and zero evidence that he made anything more efficient. DOGE was quietly disbanded after his departure, with its functions absorbed by OMB. The wreckage he left behind was not quiet at all.

## The Pattern Is the Point

In any single domain, you could argue these are growing pains. Immigration enforcement is complex. Foreign policy is messy. Military transitions are disruptive. Government reform is hard.

But across every domain, the failure mode is identical: act first, discover consequences later, blame someone else, repeat.

Deport first, check the legal order later. Announce tariffs first, model the economic impact never. Share classified strike plans first, consider operational security after the journalist publishes it. Fire the workforce first, ask what they actually did after the systems crash. Strike Iran first, figure out how to keep the oil flowing after the mines are already in the water. Cut the aid budget first, count the dead later. Delete the Black Medal of Honor recipient first, ask why the AI flagged him after the outrage.

Let's be clear: this is not a policy disagreement. This is the absence of policy — except where it concerns the systematic targeting of Black and brown Americans, where the planning is remarkably consistent. It is governing by press conference in every domain except racial hierarchy, where the executive orders are precise, the enforcement is coordinated, and the rhetoric and the machinery move in lockstep. It is the organizational equivalent of a contractor who demolishes load-bearing walls because they looked unnecessary, blames the architect when the ceiling falls in, and then bills the Black family next door for the debris removal.

The courts have noticed. In the first 100 days alone, district courts issued approximately 25 universal injunctions against Trump administration policies. The birthright citizenship executive order was blocked by three separate federal courts. More than 100 lawsuits and 50 restraining orders were filed over foreign student visa revocations before the administration reversed course. A federal judge ordered the government to stop mass firings of probationary employees and rehire all who had been fired. When every branch of the judiciary is telling you to stop, you are not governing. You are flailing.

There is a word for what happens when an institution systematically refuses to think past the next news cycle: decay. Institutions do not collapse in a single dramatic moment. They erode..through neglect, through arrogance, through the slow replacement of expertise with loyalty and planning with impulse. The Eisenhower administration built the Interstate Highway System by spending years on engineering studies before breaking ground. The Marshall Plan rebuilt Europe through meticulous economic planning and coalition coordination. These were not cautious administrations — they were ambitious ones. But they understood that ambition without forethought is just destruction with good branding.

We have watched this administration deport people to the wrong countries, crash the systems that deliver Social Security benefits, alienate the allies we need, share military secrets on family group chats, tank markets with unmodeled tariff announcements, gut the agencies that keep planes in the air and forecast hurricanes, launch strikes on Iran after scrapping the only ships that could keep Hormuz open, dismantle a century of civil rights infrastructure, delete Black heroes from military history, strip legal status from half a million refugees targeted by a neo-Nazi lie, call sitting members of Congress "garbage," quote Hitler at rallies, and boast about feeding humanitarian aid into a wood chipper while millions lose access to medicine — all while claiming to be the most effective administration in history.

The record says otherwise. And the record does not care about branding.

## What We Owe the Future

We are not spectators. Every one of these failures has a cost measured in human lives, economic security, institutional trust, and national credibility. A man sits in a Salvadoran prison because nobody read the court order. Seniors cannot access their benefits because nobody modeled what happens when you fire the people who process the claims. Pilots fly missions whose details were shared on a group chat with the Defense Secretary's brother. A soccer player rots in a mega-prison because someone mistook a Real Madrid tattoo for a gang sign. Two pilots are dead at LaGuardia because one controller was doing two jobs. The world's most critical oil chokepoint is mined shut because nobody thought to keep the minesweepers in theater before launching the strikes that made mining inevitable. Black children in Alabama still play in yards with raw sewage because the government reclassified their suffering as a "DEI" program and walked away. A century of stolen farmland was declared "sufficiently addressed." The first Black Medal of Honor recipient was deleted from a government website by an AI that was told to find diversity and found heroism instead. And 9.4 million people may die by the end of the decade because a billionaire thought humanitarian aid was a punchline.

The question is not whether this administration lacks forethought. The evidence has answered that. The question is not whether this administration is racist. The evidence has answered that, too — in the president's own words, in his own Cabinet meetings, echoing language from the darkest chapter in European history while his enforcement agencies carry out the targeting he describes. The question is what we intend to do about a government that treats planning as weakness, consequences as someone else's problem, and racial hierarchy as an organizing principle.

Democracies do not survive on autopilot. They require citizens who demand competence, not just spectacle. Who insist that policy be more than an announcement. Who understand that the purpose of government is not to perform strength but to exercise it — carefully, deliberately, and with full knowledge of what comes next. And who refuse — categorically, without equivocation — to let their government sort its citizens by blood and skin and origin while calling it patriotism.

This is one of those periods in our history that future generations will study. They will ask whether we demanded better — whether we held our leaders to the basic standard of thinking before acting. Whether we recognized fascism when it arrived wearing a flag pin and speaking in eugenics. Or whether we let the spectacle distract us while the machinery of self-governance was stripped for parts by people who never bothered to learn what it did — and handed to people who knew exactly what they intended to do with it.

We owe that answer to the next generation. We owe it to ourselves. And we owe it to every person already paying the price for an administration that never learned to think past tomorrow — and never intended to think past its own power.
